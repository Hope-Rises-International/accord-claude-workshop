1

Describe the Friction, Not the Solution

When you don’t know how to solve something, the temptation is to guess at a solution and ask Claude to build that. It almost always backfires. Describe what’s hard instead, and let Claude shape the answer.

This is the single most important habit when you sit down with Claude. The instinct is to walk in with a solution already in mind — “build me a dashboard,” “make me a database.” But the moment you name a solution, you’ve locked Claude into your guess about what’s possible. Describe the friction, and you open the whole solution space.

Same situation, two ways to ask

✗ Solution-shaped
“Build me a grants-management database with a calendar view and email reminders.”

Locks the answer in before the conversation starts. You end up debugging a tool you may not have needed.
✓ Friction-shaped
“We have grants from 14 funders this year. Each has a different reporting deadline and format. Last quarter I missed one because it was buried in an email thread. I have no central place that tells me what’s due when.”

Describes what’s real. Now Claude can push back, refine, or suggest something you’d never have asked for.
Try it now — your real friction

Open a chat in your Accord project. Think of one thing in your week that’s slow, manual, or brittle — member onboarding, convening logistics, chasing data from member orgs, prepping the OneAccord Forum, the CRDA Journal pipeline. Describe it the way you’d complain to a colleague. Don’t propose a fix. Then send it and see what Claude does with it.

Expect Claude to push past the symptom

The thing that hurts most is rarely the real problem. You describe the slow part; Claude often diagnoses the structural cause underneath it — sometimes proposing a process change instead of a tool. That redirect is the value, not a misunderstanding. Welcome it. (This is Toyota’s old “5 Whys” discipline: keep asking why until the cause behind the symptom appears.)

Everyday friction vs. the big ones

For an everyday bottleneck, describe-and-iterate is enough — that’s what we just did. For something larger, like rebuilding a whole workflow, there’s a heavier, step-by-step method worth following. That’s the next block.

And know when Claude isn’t the right tool

Heavy statistical work with survey weights? Use a real stats package with Claude as your support analyst. Rendering images? That’s the next block. The skill is knowing when Claude is the executor versus when it’s the guide pointing you to the right tool.

“You don’t need to know how to use Claude to complain to it.” Tell it what’s hard, what takes too long, what form you wish would die. That’s the entire skill in one sentence.

2

A Method for the Big Problems

For an everyday task, a good prompt is enough. For a big one — rebuilding how something actually works — what you need isn’t a smarter prompt. It’s a disciplined process. Six steps.

Block 1 was the everyday move: describe the friction, let Claude shape an answer, iterate. That’s plenty for most of your week. But some problems are bigger — rebuilding how Accord gathers and synthesizes data from 160+ member organizations, or rethinking the CRDA Journal pipeline end to end. For those, don’t expect the first answer to be right, and don’t accept it. Run it through a loop that forces research, refinement, and an outside opinion before you commit.

Step 1 — Describe the friction

Friction-shaped, no solution — exactly like Block 1. Start from what’s hard, not from a tool you’ve already imagined.

Step 2 — Let Claude propose — but don’t accept it yet

The first proposal is a starting point, not the answer. Read it, then hold it loosely. The work is just beginning.

Step 3 — Send Claude to research the field

This is the step most people skip. Have Claude go and actually read the world — current standards, what peer networks do, what the field considers best practice — before it commits to anything.

Before you finalize a recommendation, go research this properly. Read what’s current and standard in the relief-and-development field, look at how comparable networks handle it, and cite your sources. Then come back and tell me what you learned and how it changes your proposal.
Step 4 — Have Claude refine its proposal

Now that it has read the field, ask it to rebuild the recommendation in light of what it just learned. The second version is almost always materially better than the first.

Step 5 — Get an outside critique

Take the refined proposal to a different AI — Gemini, ChatGPT, either. Ask it to find the flaws. Independent eyes catch what one model misses.

Step 6 — Finalize, then ask humans

Bring the critique back to Claude, have it onboard the feedback and produce a final version — then put it in front of your team. The AI sharpens the thinking; people still make the call.

Why run it past a second AI?

Different models notice different things. A second model sees the proposal with fresh eyes and flags what Claude was too close to catch. It’s the AI version of asking a trusted colleague for a second opinion before you commit — cheap insurance on a decision that matters.

When to reach for this

Don’t use the heavy loop on everyday friction — that’s overkill, and Block 1’s describe-and-iterate is faster. Save these six steps for the big, structural problems where getting it wrong is expensive: a new operating model, a major funder strategy, a process the whole network will depend on.

Try it — one real big problem

Pick one genuinely large thing on your plate at Accord. In your project, run it through all six steps in a single sitting. Notice how different the Step 4 answer is from the Step 2 answer — that gap is the research doing its work.

3

Use the Right Tool — and Combine Them

The best output doesn’t come from forcing one tool to do everything. It comes from giving each tool the job it’s best at. The tool that thinks about the output and the tool that renders it don’t have to be the same tool.

Claude is a language model — it thinks in words, not pixels. It can describe exactly what an image should look like, down to composition, color, and mood. But turning that description into an actual image takes a different kind of model. That’s not a weakness; it’s a division of labor.

The pattern: Claude designs, another tool renders

RoleToolWhat it does
DirectorClaudeDesigns the structure, writes the image prompts, assembles the final product
Image creatorGemini (or ChatGPT)Generates high-quality images from Claude’s descriptions
AssemblerClaudeTakes the finished images and builds the polished deliverable
Try it — a visual on Accord’s Principles

In your Accord project, paste:

I need a 4-slide visual overview of the Accord Network’s Principles of Practice. For each slide, give me: the slide title, the key message in 1–2 sentences, and a detailed image description I can paste into an image AI to generate the visual. Give me the image descriptions as separate blocks I can copy one at a time.

Take each image description to gemini.google.com → “Create an image,” paste, download. (Tip: after you get an image you like, tell Gemini “in the same style” for the next one — that keeps your visuals consistent.) Then drag the images back into Claude: “Take these, add them to the overview, and finalize it for me.”

Iterate in text, build once

Here’s the trap: Claude can build a file bigger than it can read back — like a printer that prints a 500-page book but only has a 50-page scanner. A finished presentation with embedded images can eat 60–70% of the context window, so editing it over and over burns through your room fast. The fix: get all your content right in plain text first, then generate the finished deliverable once. It’ll be close to right on the first build.

Why this matters beyond images

The same principle applies to any format-heavy job — a brand-perfect slide deck, a complex spreadsheet, a long formatted report. Asking Claude to render and perfect the format in one pass means dozens of tool calls and a degrading context window, and the output drifts — silently. Let Claude structure the content; let a tool built for the format handle the rendering. Design the handoff between them. That’s the skill that compounds.

4

Why Cowork: Hand Off the Whole Task

Everything so far has been AI-assisted work: you ask, Claude answers, you go do it. Cowork is the first step into AI-completed work — you give a goal, it makes a plan, it does the in-between, it reports back.

First, plainly: what is Cowork?

Cowork is a mode inside the Claude desktop app — included in your Accord Teams plan, nothing extra to buy. It’s the same Claude you’ve been using in the browser, but with permission to actually do the work on your computer: open and organize files, fill in spreadsheets, draft into Gmail, work across a whole folder. Chat tells you how; Cowork goes and does it. (The desktop app also has a Code mode for developers — not today.)

The simplest definition of an agent: a coworker you hand a task to. “Agentic” just means the AI does the work — not only tells you how to do it yourself. The non-technical anchor is an intern who has read everything on the internet, never sleeps, and does the boring middle part for you. You still say what you want. You still review the work. They just do the in-between.

Three things that make something an agent

  • It plans before acting. It looks at your goal, figures out the steps, and shows you the plan before it touches anything. If the plan is wrong, you stop it before any damage is done.
  • It uses tools. It reads your files, browses the web, drafts emails, fills spreadsheets — the same things you do with a mouse and keyboard. That’s the difference between describing a deliverable and producing one.
  • It works in steps and reports back. Not one answer in one go. Step by step, and at any point you can say “stop,” “redo that,” or “keep going.”

Every Cowork task runs on the same four beats

Beat 1

Goal

You describe the outcome in plain English. Not the steps — the result.

Beat 2

Plan

Cowork shows what it’s about to do. You approve, edit, or cancel.

Beat 3

Work

Cowork executes. You watch, walk away, or interrupt.

Beat 4

Deliverable

It hands back the result. You review, accept, or send it back.

Your judgment lives at three points: the goal (what do you actually want?), the plan approval (will this touch the right things?), and the deliverable review (is this correct? anything missing?). You’re not being replaced — you’re being relieved of the in-between.

Goal, not instruction

✗ Instruction-shaped
“First open the folder. Then read each file’s date. Then sort by month. Then create subfolders. Then move the files. Don’t touch the videos.”

If you forget a step, you find out when the deliverable is wrong.
✓ Goal-shaped
“Sort this folder into subfolders by month. Leave videos in place.”

Cowork makes the plan and shows it to you — you catch its assumptions before any file moves.

Sort. Turn. Triage. You’ll be surprised how good the result is from a short prompt. The same shape works for real Accord work: “Draft twelve versions of this member announcement, one per recipient, customized to each org’s focus, and leave them in my drafts.” Goal in, twelve drafts out.

The memory flywheel — the sentence that turns one win into a habit

When a Cowork task goes well and you want it to happen the same way next time, say so explicitly: “Make sure we do it this way every time,” or “Remember this.” Cowork doesn’t reliably save to memory on its own — you have to enforce it the first few times. Once you do, it stops reinventing the approach and reuses the one you approved. That’s the flywheel.

The safety floor: the default is nothing

Cowork can only see what you’ve explicitly let it see — every folder is a separate grant; it cannot wander your machine. And it won’t send an email or unsubscribe you from a list on its own. It drafts; you send. Keep it that way.

Put it on a schedule

Anything you can ask Cowork to do once, you can ask it to do on a schedule — a Monday-morning brief of your week’s member calls, a weekly folder cleanup. One catch, worth knowing: scheduled tasks only run when your computer is on, awake, and Claude is open. Turn on the Keep-Awake setting in the schedule pane so a 6 a.m. job actually fires.

Keep a Clean Workshop: Canonical Docs & Memory

Now that Claude is creating real files for you, two things keep it from becoming a mess: knowing where the true version of a document lives, and knowing what Claude actually remembers.

Canonical documents — one true version

“Canonical” means the one authoritative, current version everything else refers back to — like the canon of Scripture, or the official version of any text. The risk, now that you’re all working faster: a draft gets downloaded, edited locally, and never makes it back. Over weeks you end up with two versions of the truth — the one in your shared drive and the one on someone’s laptop. That’s drift, and it’s the thing to prevent.

#HabitWhen to use it
1Don’t download in the first place. Work in your shared drive; Claude’s connector reads it directly.Anything cloud-native (Google Docs/Sheets/Slides). Drift is impossible if no copy ever leaves.
2Ask Cowork to save it back. One sentence: “Save this to the shared drive in the [folder name] folder.”Files Cowork just created locally. Use the same folder the source materials came from.

One honest limitation

Claude’s Drive connector can read, copy, and create — but it can’t move, rename, or delete. If you’re updating an existing canonical file, it uploads a new version next to the old one; swapping them is a quick manual step in Drive. The principle holds: your job is to say what good looks like and let the workflow do the housekeeping.

What Claude actually remembers

Memory is a place, not a magic property. If it wasn’t written down somewhere, Claude doesn’t have it. And memory in one product does not cross into another — this one rule prevents most of the confusion.

Three separate buckets — what’s saved in one never reaches another.

Where you’re workingWhat it remembers across sessions
Claude.ai (web)An auto-summary of past chats, plus anything you save yourself. The richest persistent memory.
Cowork — standalone taskNothing. Each task starts fresh and forgets when it closes.
Cowork — inside a ProjectTopic-by-topic memory plus skills, carried across sessions. The sweet spot.

One project to rule them all

If you want Cowork to remember an area of your work, do that work inside one Cowork project — not scattered standalone tasks. The trade-off in one line: less memory, more power; more memory, less power. A Cowork project is the balance point — most of the persistent memory of Claude.ai, plus the extra power of Cowork. Pick the project and stay in it.

Three always-on layers you can set

Separate from per-session memory, three layers of standing context load every time, broadest to narrowest: (1) your Claude.ai Profile instructions (Settings → General — who you are, your role, your output style); (2) Cowork Global Instructions (conventions that apply everywhere in Cowork — your team roster, defaults); (3) Project Instructions (rules for one project only, e.g. “treat everything here as confidential”). And note: your Instructions box is what you tell Claude; Memory is separate — it’s what Claude learns about you on its own. Make sure memory is turned on under Settings → Capabilities.
